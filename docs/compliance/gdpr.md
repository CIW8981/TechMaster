# General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

## Legal Recognition

GDPR explicitly covers data that makes someone "identifiable" - not just "identified." This includes:
• Data that could identify someone with reasonable effort
• Information that becomes identifying when combined with other data
• Data that reveals personal characteristics or behaviors

## What is the core problem GDPR solves?

• What risks do individuals face when organizations collect their personal data?

    - Banking details, credit information used for unauthorized transactions
    - Intimate details exposed without consent, affecting personal relationships and reputation
    - Location data, contact information, or behavioral patterns enabling unwanted contact
    - Behavioral data used to influence decisions through targeted content - Personal information exposed to criminals through poor security

## What constitutes personal data?

• What makes information "personal"?

    ## Direct Identification
    • **Name and identifiers** - Full names, ID numbers, passport numbers directly point to someone
    • **Contact information** - Email addresses, phone numbers, physical addresses
    • **Biometric data** - Fingerprints, facial recognition data, DNA profiles uniquely identify individuals

    ## Indirect Identification
    • **Combination of attributes** - Age + zip code + gender might identify someone in small populations
    • **Behavioral patterns** - Shopping habits, location patterns, browsing history can be unique fingerprints
    • **Device identifiers** - IP addresses, device IDs, cookies linked to individual usage

## When is data processing justified?

• What makes collecting someone's data legitimate?

    ## Six Legal Bases

    Consent - Person explicitly agrees to specific data use
    • Must be informed, specific, and withdrawable
    • Good for: marketing, optional features, research

    Contract - Data needed to deliver requested service
    • Examples: shipping address, payment info, account access

    Legal Obligation - Required by law
    • Examples: tax records, court orders, regulatory compliance

    Vital Interests - Life-or-death situations
    • Examples: medical emergencies, child protection, disaster response

    Public Task - Official government functions
    • Examples: public services, law enforcement, elections

    Legitimate Interests - Balanced business needs
    • Must pass three-part test: legitimate purpose + necessity + balancing individual rights
    • Examples: fraud prevention, security, customer service

    ## Key Requirements
    • **Proportional** - Only collect what's needed
    • **Transparent** - Clear about legal basis and purpose
    • **Specific** - Defined reasons, not vague business purposes
    • **Documented** - Justify the chosen legal basis

    ## Invalid Approaches
    • Assumed consent from silence
    • Bundling unrelated data collection with essential services
    • Collecting "just in case" without clear purpose
    • Choosing legal basis after collection starts

## For company portal tracking is mandatory
### GDPR Approach for Mandatory Tracking

Legal Basis: Legitimate Interests
• Security monitoring and system administration
• Compliance with internal policies
• IT resource management
• Audit trail requirements

No Consent Required When:
• Tracking is essential for business operations
• You have legitimate business interests
• Employee privacy rights don't override business needs

## What about cross-border data flows?

• is GDPR need to follow in all countries? ## The question isn't "which countries require GDPR" but "does your organization process EU personal data?" If yes, GDPR likely applies to you.

    ## Where GDPR Applies
    EU/EEA Countries - Full application:
    • All 27 EU member states
    • Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway (EEA)
    • UK has similar law (UK GDPR) post-Brexit

    Non-EU Organizations - Must comply if:
    • **Offering goods/services** to people in EU (even if free)
    • **Monitoring behavior** of people in EU (tracking, profiling)
    • **Processing EU residents' data** for above purposes

    ## Global Impact Examples
    • US company with EU customers → GDPR applies
    • Chinese app used by Europeans → GDPR applies
    • Indian call center processing EU data → GDPR applies
    • Australian website blocking EU visitors → GDPR doesn't apply

    ## Countries with Similar Laws
    Many countries created GDPR-inspired laws:
    • **Brazil** - LGPD
    • **California** - CCPA/CPRA
    • **Canada** - PIPEDA updates
    • **India** - Personal Data Protection Bill
    • **South Africa** - POPIA
    • **Japan, South Korea, Singapore** - Enhanced privacy laws
